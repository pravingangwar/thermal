01 — Executive Summary

Study Overview

Objective

Compare peak junction temperature of graphite and copper heat spreaders across a thickness sweep (0.01–5 mm) using a custom Python FDM solver. Identify the crossover point and the thickness of diminishing thermal returns for each material.

Configuration

A 6 W heat source (5 mm radius) dissipates through a TIM → spreader → Adhesive → Glass stack. Graphite is highly anisotropic (kr = 1500, kz = 2 W/m·K); Copper is isotropic (k = 400 W/m·K). TIM footprint matches the source footprint only (r ≤ 5 mm). Cooling by natural convection (h = 10 W/m²K) and radiation (ε = 0.9) on glass back surface only.

Key Finding

Graphite significantly outperforms Copper below ~0.75 mm due to its 1500 W/m·K in-plane conductivity. The curves converge near 0.75 mm and invert beyond 1 mm where Copper's isotropic conductivity becomes an advantage. Above 0.4 mm both materials plateau — glass layer resistance dominates.

Cross-Check

FDM results are cross-checked against Siemens FLOEFD thermal simulation at t = 0.1, 0.4, and 1.0 mm for both materials. Peak temperature agreement within 1–2% on a ΔT above ambient basis at the validated operating point.

02 — Geometry & Material Stack

Thermal Stack Definition

Single unified r-z grid covering 0 ≤ r ≤ Rgl = 50 mm, 0 ≤ z ≤ H = 1.3 mm. TIM footprint is restricted to the source radius (r < 5 mm); the region r ≥ 5 mm at the TIM z-level is treated as air. For r > Rsp below the glass base, void nodes represent the air gap (adiabatic bottom BC).

03 — Numerical Methodology

FDM Solver Architecture

Discretisation

Cylindrical coordinates (r, z). Interior nodes use a finite volume formulation with harmonic-mean interface conductivities to handle material discontinuities. Axis symmetry at r = 0 is enforced via a L'Hôpital limit giving c_rp = 2k/Δr².

Nonlinear Radiation BC

Top surface radiation is linearised at each Newton iteration using hrad = 4σεT³. Combined convection + radiation flux is assembled directly into the sparse matrix at j = Nz−1, avoiding separate radiation iteration. Converges in 4–6 Newton steps to 10⁻⁴ °C.

Sparse Linear System

Assembled as a SciPy lil_matrix converted to CSR and solved with spsolve. Typical DOF: ~90,000.

Anisotropic Graphite

kr = 1500 W/m·K (in-plane) vs kz = 2 W/m·K (through-plane) — a 750× anisotropy ratio. Heat spreads radially at near-isothermal conditions before conducting axially into adhesive and glass.

Top Surface BC — Newton Linearisation

# Radiation linearised as h_rad = 4σεT³ at previous Newton iterate
T0_K   = T_prev[-1, i] + 273.15
q_rad0 = σ · ε · (T0_K⁴ − Ts_K⁴)
h_rad  = 4 · σ · ε · T0_K³

# Matrix row at (Nz-1, i) — combined conv + rad flux balance:
A[n, Nz-2] =  kzc / dz
A[n, Nz-1] = -(kzc / dz + h_conv + h_rad)
b[n]        = -(h_conv · T_amb + h_rad · T0_C − q_rad0)

Key Observations

Graphite outperforms Copper below ~0.75 mm thickness due to its exceptionally high in-plane conductivity (1500 vs 400 W/m·K). Above ~0.75 mm, Copper's isotropic conductivity provides better through-plane transport and the curves cross over.

Both materials show diminishing returns above 0.4 mm — the glass layer thermal resistance becomes the bottleneck beyond this point.

Residual Difference Sources

Remaining sub-2% agreement is consistent with differences in solver approach:

  • FDM uses uniform h = 10 W/m²K; FLOEFD computes local variation
  • Harmonic-mean interface conductivity vs FLOEFD volumetric interpolation
  • Grid resolution at the 5 mm source boundary flux step
  • FDM axisymmetric vs FLOEFD half-symmetry cut plane treatment
